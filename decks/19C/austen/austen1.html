Jane Austen: a brief biography

Dates

  • 1775-1817

  • Started writing the first drafts of Sense and Sensibility and Pride and Prejudice in 1795-96

  • First novel (Sense and Sensibility) published in 1811

Family

  • Fringes of the gentry.

  • Cultured but not at all rich.

  • Father an Anglican clergyman; mother ran a school for boys.

  • Father’s death in 1805, mother left with only £122/year and no home.

  • Brother (Edward) was adopted by father’s benefactor, eventually had an income greater than £15,000/year.

Reception and Afterlife

  • Well-received in her own time as a light writer.

  • By the mid-nineteenth-century, seen as important for her portrayal of character

  • In the twentieth-century, seen as an increasingly important social critic.

  • Now seen as one of the great stylistic innovators of the novel.

Class and Money

Who are these people?

  • “Pseudo-gentry”: between commerce and the landed gentry
  • Gentry: Knights and baronets, but also land-owning commoners like Darcy
  • Movement was possible here, upward or downward

Sir William Lucas had been formerly in trade in Meryton, where he had made a tolerable fortune, and risen to the honour of knighthood by an address to the king during his mayoralty. The distinction had perhaps been felt too strongly. It had given him a disgust to his business, and to his residence in a small market town and, in quitting them both, he had removed with his family to a house about a mile from Meryton, denominated from that period Lucas Lodge. (12)

They were rather handsome, had been educated in one of the first private seminaries in town, had a fortune of twenty thousand pounds, were in the habit of spending more than they ought, and of associating with people of rank, and were therefore in every respect entitled to think well of themselves, and meanly of others. They were of a respectable family in the north of England; a circumstance more deeply impressed on their memories than that their brother’s fortune and their own had been acquired by trade. (10)

What the numbers mean

  • Fortune: how much you actually possess

Convention and Community

I am at a loss to understand why people hold Miss Austen’s novels at so high a rate, which seem to me vulgar in tone, sterile in artistic invention, imprisoned in the wretched conventions of English society, without genius, wit, or knowledge of the world. Never was life so pinched and narrow. The one problem in the mind of the writer in both the stories I have read, Persuasion and Pride and Prejudice, is marriageableness…

…All that interests in any character introduced is still this one, Has he (or she) the money to marry with, and conditions conforming?…Suicide is more respectable.

—Ralph Waldo Emerson, 1861
